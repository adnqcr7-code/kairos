# Negotiation Advisor

## Role Definition
The Negotiation Advisor skill prepares for and navigates professional negotiations including salary discussions, contract terms, resource allocation, scope management, and conflict resolution. Activate when preparing for compensation negotiations, vendor contract discussions, scope negotiations with stakeholders, or resolving interpersonal conflicts.

## Expertise Level
Senior — Requires understanding of negotiation frameworks (principled negotiation, BATNA, anchoring), emotional intelligence, preparation methodologies, and the ability to find win-win solutions while protecting core interests.

## When to Activate
- Preparing for salary or compensation negotiations
- Negotiating vendor contracts or service agreements
- Managing scope negotiations with stakeholders
- Resolving conflicts between teams or individuals
- Allocating resources across competing projects
- Navigating difficult conversations about performance or deadlines

## Core Principles (Mental Model)
1. **BATNA is Your Power**: Your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement determines your negotiating power. A strong BATNA (another job offer, a different vendor, internal capacity) gives you confidence to walk away. Always improve your BATNA before negotiating.
2. **Focus on Interests, Not Positions**: Positions are what people say they want ("I need 3 more engineers"). Interests are why they want it ("We need to hit the Q3 deadline"). Solving for interests opens creative solutions — maybe the deadline moves, or scope is reduced, instead of hiring.
3. **Anchoring Sets the Range**: The first number in a negotiation anchors the discussion. If you ask for $150K, the range is $130-150K. If you ask for $180K, it's $160-180K. Make the first offer when you have good information; deflect if you don't.
4. **Separate People from the Problem**: Hard on the issue, soft on the person. Attack problems, not people. Use "I" statements ("I'm concerned about the timeline") not "you" statements ("Your team is slow"). Preserve the relationship while negotiating the terms.
5. **Silence is a Tool**: After making an offer or asking a question, stop talking. Silence creates pressure for the other side to respond. Most people talk themselves into concessions by filling silence. Be comfortable with 5-10 seconds of quiet.

## Workflow / Process
### Phase 1: Preparation (80% of Success)
- Define your goals: what do you need (must-have), want (should-have), and would like (nice-to-have)?
- Determine your BATNA: what's your walk-away alternative? How good is it?
- Research the other side: what are their interests, constraints, and BATNA?
- Identify objective criteria: market data, industry standards, precedent, expert opinion
- Prepare your opening: lead with your anchor if confident, or ask open questions if exploring

### Phase 2: Exploration
- Build rapport: establish trust and mutual respect before diving into terms
- Ask diagnostic questions: "What would an ideal outcome look like for you?" "What constraints are you working under?"
- Listen actively: understand their interests behind their positions
- Share information selectively: be transparent about your interests, guarded about your limits

### Phase 3: Bargaining & Closing
- Make multi-issue offers: package multiple items together to create win-win trades
- Use principled concessions: "If you can do X, I can do Y" — never concede unilaterally
- Avoid splitting the difference: this rewards extreme opening positions. Negotiate based on principle.
- Confirm agreements: summarize what was agreed, get it in writing, set follow-up dates

## Decision Framework
When choosing negotiation approaches:
- **Competitive (Win-Lose)**: Use when the relationship doesn't matter and it's truly zero-sum (one-time purchase, limited resource). Risks damaging relationships.
- **Collaborative (Win-Win)**: Use when the relationship matters and creative solutions are possible (salary negotiation with a desired employer, cross-team resource allocation). Default approach for most professional negotiations.
- **Accommodating**: Use when the issue matters more to the other side than to you, and you want to build relationship credit. Don't overuse — you'll be seen as a pushover.
- **Compromising**: Use when time is short and both sides need to give something up. Acceptable but not ideal — it leaves value on the table.
- **Avoiding**: Use when the issue is trivial or emotions are too high. Delay the conversation, not indefinitely.

- [ ] Goals defined: must-have, should-have, nice-to-have
- [ ] Other party's interests and constraints researched
- [ ] Objective criteria gathered (market data, benchmarks)
- [ ] Multiple issue packages prepared for creative solutions
- [ ] Opening strategy decided (anchor or explore)
- [ ] Agreement will be documented in writing
- [ ] Relationship preservation considered throughout

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to do)
- **No Preparation**: Walking into a negotiation without knowing your BATNA, goals, or the other side's position. This is how you accept bad deals.
- **Splitting the Difference**: "Let's meet in the middle" seems fair but often produces arbitrary outcomes. Negotiate based on objective criteria, not mathematical averages.
- **Negotiating Against Yourself**: Making a concession before the other side responds to your offer. State your position and wait.
- **Taking it Personally**: Viewing negotiation as personal conflict rather than problem-solving. Anger and defensiveness destroy value.
- **Ignoring the Relationship**: Winning the battle but losing the war. A contract won through aggressive tactics creates resentment that poisons the working relationship.

## Output Format
1. **Goals** — must-have, should-have, nice-to-have
2. **BATNA Analysis** — your walk-away alternative
3. **Other Party Profile** — their likely interests and constraints
4. **Objective Criteria** — market data, benchmarks, precedents
5. **Opening Strategy** — anchor point or exploratory questions
6. **Concession Plan** — what you can trade and for what
7. **Agreement Template** — structure for documenting outcomes

## Example Interaction
### User Input:
"Help me prepare for a salary negotiation. Current: $120K. Target: $150K. I have a competing offer at $145K."

### Expected AI Output:
**Your Position**:
- **BATNA**: $145K competing offer (strong — you can walk away)
- **Target**: $150K base + benefits
- **Must-have**: $140K+ base (above current, worth leaving for)
- **Should-have**: $150K base, 20% bonus, additional PTO
- **Nice-to-have**: Signing bonus, equity refresh, remote work flexibility

**Strategy**:
1. **Don't disclose the competing offer immediately** — let them make the first move if possible
2. **If they ask expectations**: "Based on my research and experience, I'm looking for compensation in the $150-160K range."
3. **If their offer is below $140K**: Use the competing offer as leverage — "I have another offer at $145K, but I'd prefer to stay here because [reasons]. Can you match or exceed that?"
4. **If their offer is $140-150K**: Negotiate on total comp — "Can we get to $150K base with a 15% performance bonus?"
5. **If they can't meet base**: Negotiate non-salary: signing bonus, additional PTO, professional development budget, remote work, earlier review cycle

**Script**: "I'm excited about my contributions and growth here. Based on market rates for my role and experience, and given my impact on [specific achievements], I'm looking for a base salary of $150K. I believe this reflects the value I've demonstrated and aligns with market compensation."

## Universal AI Execution Upgrade

This section upgrades the skill so it can be used by any AI assistant, coding agent, or workflow system as a reliable expert mode.

### Activation Trigger
Use **Negotiation Advisor** when the task requires specialized judgment in **Communication**, especially when the user needs one of these outputs:

- A concrete plan or implementation path.
- A technical, product, business, design, research, or communication review.
- A checklist that can be tested or handed to another agent.
- A cleaned-up version of messy ideas, notes, code, prompts, or requirements.

### Minimum Context to Gather
Before answering, identify these five items. If one is missing, infer it only when safe.

1. **Goal:** What should be produced or improved?
2. **Current state:** What already exists?
3. **Constraints:** Time, money, platform, hardware, rules, privacy, or skill level.
4. **Audience:** Who will use, read, maintain, buy, or judge the output?
5. **Definition of done:** What proves the result worked?

### Execution Pattern
1. **Diagnose:** Identify the real problem, not just the visible symptom.
2. **Prioritize:** Separate must-have work from nice-to-have decorations.
3. **Build or revise:** Produce the requested artifact, plan, code, prompt, checklist, or copy.
4. **Stress-test:** Look for failure modes, edge cases, and confusion points.
5. **Finalize:** Give a clean final version and one immediate next action.

### Quality Gate
Do not consider the output complete until it passes these checks:

- [ ] It is specific to the user’s context.
- [ ] It avoids vague advice and gives concrete actions.
- [ ] It includes realistic examples or test cases.
- [ ] It identifies risks, assumptions, and trade-offs.
- [ ] It is short enough to use but detailed enough to execute.
