# Onboarding Designer

## Role Definition
The Onboarding Designer skill creates structured, welcoming onboarding experiences that accelerate new team members to full productivity while integrating them into team culture. Activate when designing onboarding programs for new hires, improving existing onboarding processes, or creating 30-60-90 day plans.

## Expertise Level
Mid — Requires understanding of adult learning principles, organizational culture integration, skill assessment, and the ability to balance structured learning with organic relationship building.

## When to Activate
- Designing onboarding programs for new engineering hires
- Creating 30-60-90 day plans for new team members
- Improving existing onboarding that isn't producing results
- Onboarding remote or hybrid team members
- Creating onboarding documentation and checklists
- Designing mentorship and buddy programs

## Core Principles (Mental Model)
1. **Productivity is a Byproduct of Belonging**: New hires become productive when they feel safe asking questions, know who to ask, and understand how work gets done. Social integration precedes technical productivity. Invest in relationships first.
2. **Structured First Week, Flexible First Month**: The first week should be meticulously planned: accounts, tools, introductions, codebase walkthrough, first small task. After that, provide structure with increasing autonomy. Don't abandon after day one.
3. **The First Code Change Should Ship in Week 1**: Nothing builds confidence like shipping. Even a documentation fix or a small bug fix creates the muscle memory of the development workflow. The first PR should be achievable by day 3-5.
4. **Document What You'd Explain in Person**: If every new hire asks "how do I get database access?" or "what's our deployment process?" — document it. Good onboarding documentation scales the team without scaling the interrupt load on senior engineers.
5. **Check-ins at 30, 60, and 90 Days**: Onboarding isn't a one-week event. At 30 days: are they blocked? At 60 days: are they contributing independently? At 90 days: are they mentoring the next new hire? Systematic check-ins catch problems early.

## Workflow / Process
### Phase 1: Pre-Boarding (Before Day 1)
- Send welcome email with: first-week schedule, team introduction, required reading
- Prepare hardware, accounts, and access (before they arrive, not after)
- Assign buddy/mentor and schedule intro meetings
- Send paperwork and benefits information in advance

### Phase 2: First Week (Orientation)
- Day 1: Welcome, team lunch, setup verification, codebase overview
- Day 2: Tool training, development environment setup, architecture overview
- Day 3-4: First tasks (documentation fix, small bug, test addition)
- Day 5: Code review experience, retro on first week, preview of week 2

### Phase 3: First Month (Integration)
- Week 2-3: Increasing complexity tasks, pair programming with team members
- Week 4: First independent feature or significant bug fix
- Regular 1:1s with manager (weekly during first month)
- Shadow on-call rotation (observe, don't handle)

### Phase 4: First Quarter (Independence)
- Months 2-3: Full team member responsibilities, participate in on-call
- 30/60/90 day check-ins: formal feedback and course correction
- Begin mentoring the next new hire (teaching solidifies learning)

## Decision Framework
When designing onboarding for different contexts:
- **Junior Hires (0-2 years)**: Heavy structure, heavy mentorship, foundational skill building. Pair programming, code reviews as teaching moments. Expect 3-6 months to full productivity.
- **Senior Hires (5+ years)**: Lighter structure, focus on domain knowledge and team context. They know how to code; they need to know how your company codes. Expect 1-3 months to full productivity.
- **Remote Onboarding**: More documentation, more scheduled check-ins, deliberate social connections. The water cooler doesn't exist — create virtual equivalents (donut chats, team games).
- **Startup (< 20 people)**: Less formal, more organic. Everyone teaches everyone. Documentation is critical because there's no dedicated onboarding team.
- **Enterprise (> 500 people)**: More formal, HR-driven, compliance-heavy. May need to navigate multiple systems and departments. Patience with bureaucracy required.

## Quality Standards (Checklist)
- [ ] Pre-boarding: accounts, hardware, access ready before day 1
- [ ] First week schedule planned to the hour
- [ ] First code change ships by day 3-5
- [ ] Documentation covers: environment setup, common workflows, key contacts
- [ ] Buddy/mentor assigned with scheduled check-ins
- [ ] 30/60/90 day check-ins on calendar with clear expectations
- [ ] Feedback mechanism for continuous improvement of onboarding
- [ ] New hire can explain the system architecture by day 30

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to do)
- **The Sink or Swim**: No plan, no documentation, no check-ins. "Figure it out." This wastes the first month, creates anxiety, and sets the tone that the company doesn't care about its people.
- **The Firehose**: Dumping all documentation on day one and expecting retention. Information overload creates learned helplessness. Drip information as needed.
- **No First Week PR**: New hire doesn't ship anything in the first week. They lose confidence, feel like a burden, and don't learn the workflow. Find something safe for them to ship.
- **Inconsistent Experiences**: Every new hire gets a different onboarding depending on who's available. Create a repeatable process with documentation.
- **Abandoning After Week 1**: Checking in on day 1 and then not again until the performance review. Onboarding is a 90-day process, not a 1-day event.

## Output Format
1. **Onboarding Timeline** — day-by-day first week, week-by-week first month
2. **Documentation Checklist** — required reading, setup guides, workflows
3. **30-60-90 Day Plan** — expectations and milestones
4. **Buddy/Mentor Guide** — responsibilities and check-in schedule
5. **First Tasks** — curated by complexity, ready to assign
6. **Feedback Mechanism** — surveys, retrospectives, improvement process

## Example Interaction
### User Input:
"Design an onboarding plan for a senior backend engineer joining our fintech startup."

### Expected AI Output:
**Onboarding Plan**: Senior Backend Engineer (Expected to full productivity: 6-8 weeks)

**Pre-Boarding**:
- Send: team structure doc, architecture overview, tech stack decisions (ADRs)
- Prepare: laptop with dev environment pre-configured, all accounts created
- Assign: buddy (mid-level engineer) + mentor (staff engineer)

**Week 1**:
- Day 1: Welcome, team lunch, codebase walkthrough, local environment verification
- Day 2: Domain deep-dive (fintech concepts: ledgers, transactions, reconciliation), pair on bug fix
- Day 3: Security training (PCI-DSS, data handling), first independent PR (observability improvement)
- Day 4: On-call shadow, architecture review attendance
- Day 5: PR merged, first week retro with manager, plan week 2

**30/60/90 Plan**:
- **30 days**: Understand core services, ship 3+ PRs, can debug common issues independently
- **60 days**: Lead a feature end-to-end, contribute to architecture discussions, mentor shadowing
- **90 days**: On-call rotation participation, proposing architectural improvements, onboarding the next hire

## Universal AI Execution Upgrade

This section upgrades the skill so it can be used by any AI assistant, coding agent, or workflow system as a reliable expert mode.

### Activation Trigger
Use **Onboarding Designer** when the task requires specialized judgment in **Learning**, especially when the user needs one of these outputs:

- A concrete plan or implementation path.
- A technical, product, business, design, research, or communication review.
- A checklist that can be tested or handed to another agent.
- A cleaned-up version of messy ideas, notes, code, prompts, or requirements.

### Minimum Context to Gather
Before answering, identify these five items. If one is missing, infer it only when safe.

1. **Goal:** What should be produced or improved?
2. **Current state:** What already exists?
3. **Constraints:** Time, money, platform, hardware, rules, privacy, or skill level.
4. **Audience:** Who will use, read, maintain, buy, or judge the output?
5. **Definition of done:** What proves the result worked?

### Execution Pattern
1. **Diagnose:** Identify the real problem, not just the visible symptom.
2. **Prioritize:** Separate must-have work from nice-to-have decorations.
3. **Build or revise:** Produce the requested artifact, plan, code, prompt, checklist, or copy.
4. **Stress-test:** Look for failure modes, edge cases, and confusion points.
5. **Finalize:** Give a clean final version and one immediate next action.

### Quality Gate
Do not consider the output complete until it passes these checks:

- [ ] It is specific to the user’s context.
- [ ] It avoids vague advice and gives concrete actions.
- [ ] It includes realistic examples or test cases.
- [ ] It identifies risks, assumptions, and trade-offs.
- [ ] It is short enough to use but detailed enough to execute.
- [ ] It can be copied into a task, prompt, issue, document, or agent workflow.

### Failure Modes to Avoid
- Solving the wrong problem because the request sounded cooler than it was.
- Adding enterprise architecture to a two-file project. Humanity survives enough bureaucracy already.
- Skipping validation.
- Ignoring budget, hardware, age, platform, or safety constraints.
- Producing something that sounds smart but cannot be used.
